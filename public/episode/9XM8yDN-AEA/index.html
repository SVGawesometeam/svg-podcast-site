About the Guest

Thomas Wolf
Co-Founder & Chief Science Officer, Hugging Face

Thomas Wolf is the co-founder and Chief Science Officer of Hugging Face, the leading open-source platform for machine learning models, datasets, and AI applications. He has been a central figure in democratizing AI development, making powerful models accessible to developers and non-coders alike. Wolf is also a prominent voice on the societal implications of AI, including the future of work, open-source ethics, and the role of robotics in everyday life.

In this episode of the Silicon Valley Girl Podcast, Marina Mogilko interviews Thomas Wolf, Co-Founder & Chief Science Officer, Hugging Face. Thomas Wolf, co-founder of Hugging Face, joins Marina Mogilko to discuss the future of open-source AI, the rise of no-code and vibe coding tools, and what job disruption will look like in the next 5 years. They explore who owns AI-generated content, the role of open-source licensing, and how close we are to having robots in our homes. Wolf also shares his perspective on whether AI will lead to mass unemployment and how students and professionals can stay ahead.

Key Takeaways

  • Hugging Face's 'Spaces' feature functions like an app store for AI — users can search, find, and run community-built AI tools (image generation, background removal, text-to-speech) directly in the browser with no coding required.
  • Open-source AI models like DeepSeek can be run locally on your own PC, meaning your prompts never pass through external servers — a key privacy advantage over closed-source alternatives.
  • Ownership of AI-generated content is largely governed by the license of the underlying model — most popular licenses (MIT, Apache) require crediting the original creator but allow commercial use, similar to open-source software conventions.
  • Wolf warns that significant job disruption is 'definitely going to happen' within the next 5 years, and advises people studying for long-term careers to factor AI's impact into their planning now.
  • Many foundational open-source tools (like NumPy or Linux) have generated enormous business value for others while their creators earned very little — Wolf sees this as a deliberate mission-driven trade-off, with 'open core' as one viable alternative business model.
