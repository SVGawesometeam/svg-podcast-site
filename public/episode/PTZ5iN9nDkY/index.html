About the Guest

Yossi Matias
Head of Google Research

Yossi Matias is Head of Google Research with over 20 years at Google, where he built foundational products including Autocomplete, Google Trends, and Google Duplex. He has deep expertise in search, machine learning, and large language models, and actively leads hiring at one of the tech industry's most influential research organizations.

In this episode of the Silicon Valley Girl Podcast, Marina Mogilko interviews Yossi Matias, Head of Google Research. Yossi Matias, who has spent over 20 years at Google building foundational products like Autocomplete, Google Trends, and Google Duplex, argues that the AI inflection point has already passed—most people simply didn't notice because they adapted seamlessly. The conversation covers six major trends reshaping work in 2026: AI agents automating tasks across email, calendar, and CRM systems; vibe coding enabling non-technical people to build working products in days; a fundamental shift in hiring priorities away from specific technical skills; technology becoming invisible through seamless integration; AI applications in education; and solving previously impossible problems. Stanford research shows 35% of productivity gains now come from context-aware agents, while 36% of new companies are solo-founded by single entrepreneurs building what previously required teams of 12-100 people. Matias emphasizes that the critical differentiator isn't technical knowledge—it's judgment, the ability to think critically about what to ask AI and evaluate its output, combined with adaptability as tools evolve monthly.

Key Takeaways

  • The AI shift is complete—36% of new companies in 2026 are solo-founded with one person building products that previously required 12-100 person teams, up from 23% five years ago
  • AI agents executing across your entire system (email, calendar, CRM, research) deliver 35% of productivity gains, equivalent to a 4-day vs. 6-day work week difference
  • Vibe coding—describing in plain language what you want built—has eliminated the coding barrier; non-technical people are shipping working products in 2-3 days using generative UI
  • Technical skills matter less than judgment and taste—Google Research prioritizes hiring people who can think, adapt faster than technology changes, and evaluate AI outputs critically
  • Human motivation and ambition remain constant; AI is an amplifier for human ingenuity, pushing expectations upward rather than replacing work—similar to how Google Search didn't make learning obsolete but elevated it
