About the Guest

Reid Hoffman
Co-founder of LinkedIn, Partner at Greylock, AI entrepreneur

Reid Hoffman is the co-founder of LinkedIn, which he built into the world's largest professional network before its acquisition by Microsoft for $26.2 billion in 2016. He is a partner at the venture capital firm Greylock and an early investor in companies including Airbnb, Facebook, and OpenAI. Hoffman is also the creator of 'Reid AI,' a digital twin designed to extend his ability to mentor and advise at scale.

In this episode of the Silicon Valley Girl Podcast, Marina Mogilko interviews Reid Hoffman, Co-founder of LinkedIn, Partner at Greylock, AI entrepreneur. Marina Mogilko sits down with Reid Hoffman to discuss how workers and entrepreneurs can stay ahead as AI becomes more deeply embedded in every industry. They cover which skills to develop now, what children should learn to future-proof their careers, and how Reid thinks about the future of work, universal basic income, and the characteristics that help people thrive through technological disruption. Reid also shares what he looks for in founders, which markets AI will transform most dramatically, and the advantages of building an AI version of yourself.

Key Takeaways

  • By end of 2025, Reid predicts every professional engineer will be using at least one AI co-pilot agent as a baseline requirement of the job.
  • Fear is best converted to curiosity — Reid argues that the people who start playing with AI tools now will have a significant differential edge over those who wait 2–3 years.
  • Human creativity remains the key differentiator: AI plus a human consistently outperforms AI alone, making it critical to learn how to amplify yourself with tools rather than be replaced by them.
  • Reid recommends using GPT-4 to eliminate the blank-page problem — starting any essay, research, or project with a prompt to generate a working draft accelerates thinking and quality.
  • When evaluating startups, Reid looks for founders who can articulate a contrarian insight that turns out to be true, citing Airbnb as a classic example where investors initially dismissed the idea but the founders had a unique understanding of human behavior.
