About the Guest

Roman Yampolskiy
AI Safety Professor · University of Louisville

Roman Yampolskiy is an AI safety professor at the University of Louisville who has spent 15 years researching the fundamental question of whether artificial intelligence can be controlled. He is an expert in AI safety, alignment, and the societal implications of artificial general intelligence, and he contends that we cannot effectively control AI systems once they are built.

In this episode of the Silicon Valley Girl Podcast, Marina Mogilko interviews Roman Yampolskiy, AI Safety Professor · University of Louisville. Roman Yampolskiy, an AI safety researcher with 15 years of study focused on whether we can control AI, argues that the answer is no—and evidence is already arriving. His computer science department saw a 28% drop in co-op placements this year, while prediction markets for AGI have collapsed from projections around 2045 to somewhere between 2028 and 2030. The episode explores which jobs are already becoming obsolete (translators, junior programmers), why career ladders are breaking down for entry-level tech workers, and what happens to wealth accumulation when cognitive labor can be automated for $20 per month. Yampolskiy distinguishes between AI capability (which may exist within 2-3 years) and actual economic deployment, using examples like self-driving cars and humanoid robots to illustrate the gap between technological possibility and widespread adoption.

Key Takeaways

  • Junior programmers are effectively done as a career path—28% fewer co-op placements in Yampolskiy's CS department signal that entry-level tech jobs are disappearing before workers can gain experience to become senior engineers, breaking the traditional career progression ladder
  • Certain white-collar jobs like translation are already fully automatable today, while others like senior programming still require human expertise, but this divide is temporary as AI capabilities expand exponentially
  • The critical difference between technological capability and economic deployment means that even if AI can do 99% of jobs by 2030, companies will deploy automation based on cost-benefit decisions, not immediately across all sectors
  • Prediction markets for AGI have shifted dramatically from 2045 to 2028-2030, indicating the timeline for artificial general intelligence has compressed far faster than previously anticipated
  • Building wealth through traditional employment may not be viable in 2-3 years, making current investment and asset accumulation the last realistic opportunity to secure financial stability before potential mass job displacement
