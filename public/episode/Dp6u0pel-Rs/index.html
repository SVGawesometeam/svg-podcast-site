About the Guest

Saadia Zahidi
Managing Director, World Economic Forum

Saadia Zahidi is Managing Director at the World Economic Forum, where she leads work on economic growth, education, and employment. She oversees the WEF's Future of Jobs Report, a biennial study widely used by governments, corporations, and researchers to track global labor market shifts. Zahidi is also the author of 'Fifty Million Rising,' a book examining the transformation of work for women in the Muslim world.

In this episode of the Silicon Valley Girl Podcast, Marina Mogilko interviews Saadia Zahidi, Managing Director, World Economic Forum. Marina Mogilko sits down with Saadia Zahidi of the World Economic Forum to examine whether the wave of AI-attributed layoffs at companies like Block, Atlassian, and Amazon reflects genuine automation or opportunistic cost-cutting. They analyze WEF data on which jobs and tasks face the highest AI exposure by 2030 and identify the skill sets workers need to remain competitive. The episode closes with a practical 30/60/90-day action plan for future-proofing a career regardless of industry.

Key Takeaways

  • AI layoffs are partly real but also partly an excuse — insiders told Zahidi that firms are using the AI narrative as a convenient cover to correct the overhiring that occurred during the 2021–2022 boom.
  • 50% of the global workforce will need significant reskilling by 2030, according to WEF research, with white-collar roles in business, finance, law, and computer science facing the highest AI task exposure.
  • Anthropic's 2025 labor market study found AI can touch 90%+ of tasks in software engineering and data science roles, yet workers currently use AI on only a fraction of those tasks — meaning the disruption is still early.
  • Hiring into AI-exposed roles is already slowing for younger workers even before mass job losses occur, making it harder for new entrants to break into fields like software development and office administration.
  • The skills that will matter most by 2030 combine AI-native fluency, complex reasoning, and group collaboration — with Zahidi calling collaborative teamwork the most underrated career skill workers overlook.
