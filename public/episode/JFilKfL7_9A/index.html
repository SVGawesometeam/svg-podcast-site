About the Guest

Daniela Rus
Director, MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL); MIT Professor

Daniela Rus is a professor at MIT and the Director of the Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL), one of the world's largest and most influential AI research institutions. Over a 30-year career, she has pioneered research in robotics, autonomous systems, and artificial intelligence, with her work spanning soft robots, self-driving vehicles, and AI-powered hardware. She is a member of the National Academy of Engineering and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, and is widely regarded as one of the foremost authorities on the future of robotics and AI.

In this episode of the Silicon Valley Girl Podcast, Marina Mogilko interviews Daniela Rus, Director, MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL); MIT Professor. Marina Mogilko interviews MIT AI Lab Director Daniela Rus about the future of work in an AI-driven world. Rus explains that people won't lose jobs to AI itself but to colleagues who know how to use AI effectively, and outlines which skills and roles will remain valuable through 2026 and beyond. The conversation also covers edge AI, humanoid robotics, hybrid human-robot teams, and Rus's long-term vision for reversing aging.

Key Takeaways

  • People won't lose jobs to AI — they'll lose jobs to people who know how to use AI more effectively, making continuous AI training essential for anyone in a repetitive or high-volume role.
  • Edge AI (running AI directly on-device rather than in the cloud) is already here and is poised to democratize innovation the way the PC did in the 1980s, enabling individuals and small companies to build solutions without costly industrial infrastructure.
  • AI will handle the cognitive aspects of work while robots handle physical tasks, leading to hybrid human-robot teams rather than fully automated 'lights-out' factories in the near term.
  • Even in heavily automated domains like customer service chatbots, human presence remains critical for handling unanticipated edge cases that training data doesn't cover.
  • Skills tied to curiosity, creativity, strategic thinking, and human interaction are the hardest for machines to replicate and will remain the most durable career assets through at least 2030.
