About the Guest

Ankur Jain
Founder & CEO, Bilt Rewards

Ankur Jain is the founder and CEO of Bilt Rewards, a fintech startup valued at $3.1 billion that transforms rent payments into a wealth-building tool for renters. He is also known for his broader work at the intersection of housing, credit, and consumer finance, with a focus on solving systemic challenges facing younger generations. Prior to Bilt, Jain held leadership roles at Tinder and founded Kairos, a social impact-focused entrepreneurship network.

In this episode of the Silicon Valley Girl Podcast, Marina Mogilko interviews Ankur Jain, Founder & CEO, Bilt Rewards. Ankur Jain, founder of Bilt Rewards — a $3.1 billion startup — joins Marina Mogilko to explain how his platform lets renters earn airline miles, hotel points, and credit history simply by paying their monthly rent. He details how Bilt solved the classic chicken-and-egg startup problem by signing up property managers, rewards partners, and merchants simultaneously, and how COVID-19 unexpectedly accelerated their early growth. The conversation covers how Bilt points can be redeemed for flights, hotel stays, Lyft rides, and even home down payments.

Key Takeaways

  • Bilt Rewards operates at 1 in 4 apartment buildings in the U.S., automatically enrolling residents to earn points on rent payments — but anyone can sign up even if their landlord is a private individual.
  • Points are valued at an average of approximately 2.2 cents each and transfer 1-to-1 to major travel programs including United Airlines, British Airways, Emirates, Hyatt, and Hilton.
  • Bilt worked directly with regulators to enable renters to use accumulated points toward a home down payment — a first-of-its-kind solution in the industry.
  • The company spent two years pitching landlords and rewards partners before gaining traction, facing a classic cold-start network problem: property managers wanted rewards partners, and rewards partners wanted properties first.
  • COVID-19 in March 2020 became an unexpected catalyst — property managers who had previously ignored Ankur began calling back, looking for tools to drive leasing and resident retention during the crisis.
